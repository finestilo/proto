BEFORE THE ANSWERS
Image © LEDERER Timepieces
Most of the objects that define watchmaking arrive before us as finished answers.
The dial displays the time. The movement performs its function. The escapement regulates energy. The problem appears solved with observable results. And all we encounter are the answers themselves.
But what about everything that came before it?
The history of watchmaking is frequently presented as a sequence of discoveries, and inventions. Looking backwards, the path appears like one straight line. One solution follows another.
These finished results creates the impression that the destination was always clear but reality is rarely this tidy.
The people responsible for these inventions and discoveries did not encounter them as established answers. They encountered them as open problems. More importantly, they often encountered them at a moment when a perfectly acceptable answer already existed.
This is one of the more curious aspects of watchmaking. Progress does not always begin because no answer is available. Sometimes it begins because someone suspects that another answer may exist.
The first chapters of 40 Moments with Bernhard explore this territory long before innovation, independence, or chronometry become central themes. What emerges from these early recollections is not a fascination with finished watches, but a growing awareness that the same question can often produce more than one answer.
That realization appears in different forms.
A mechanical object like grandfather's watch invites a question about the mechanism behind it. A book may reveal the answer but invites another question. Historical watches can demonstrate approaches that differ from those found in modern explanations. Flea markets, museums, workshops, and collections all preserve evidence of previous attempts to address similar challenges. Taken individually, these encounters may appear unrelated. Together, they reveal something far more significant.
An accepted solution is not necessarily the only solution.
This sounds obvious when stated directly. But in practice, it changes the way a person observes the world. The objective is no longer simply to understand how something works. The objective becomes understanding why it works that way rather than another way.
The distinction is subtle but important.
Once the possibility of another answer enters the picture, observation becomes more active. Objects stop being conclusions and begin acting as evidence.
At this stage, the search remains largely intellectual.
And then reality enters the picture.
The existence of alternative answers is an interesting discovery. Pursuing them is something entirely different.
Workshops have little patience for theory that cannot function in practice. A repair waiting on the workbench does not care how elegant an idea appears in a book. Components either work or they do not. Problems arrive without warning. Instructions are not always available. Experience must fill the gaps that information cannot.
One of the recurring themes in Bernhard's recollections from this period is the realization that understanding could not simply be received. It had to be assembled.
Books were a good source of knowledge. But when put in practice the pursuit of alternative answers stops being an abstract exercise.
A question is no longer judged by how interesting it sounds. It is judged by whether it survives contact with reality.
What makes these early experiences interesting is not where they lead, but what they establish.
Long before independence, before chronometric research, and before the innovations that would later define Bernhard's work, a foundation was already forming. Not a collection of answers, but a particular way of approaching them:
The suspicion that another answer may exist.
The willingness to search for it.
And the acceptance that discovering whether it works requires considerably more effort than simply imagining it.
The first chapters of 40 Moments with Bernhard are not concerned with conclusions. They explore an earlier stage of the journey, when possibilities remain open and understanding is still being built. A stage where the most important discovery is not finding an answer, but realizing that the first answer may not be the only one.
