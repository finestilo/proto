BEFORE THE ANSWERS
Image © LEDERER Timepieces
Most of the objects that define watchmaking arrive before us as finished answers.
The dial displays the time. The movement performs its function. The escapement regulates energy. All we encounter are the observable results.
What disappears is everything that came before them.
The history of watchmaking is often presented as a sequence of discoveries and inventions. Looking backwards, the path appears straightforward. One solution follows another. One innovation improves upon the last. This representation of horological history creates the illusion that the destination was always clear.
It wasn't.
Bernhard Lederer with his 1986 Diploma Masterpiece (left).
The people responsible for these mechanisms did not encounter them as answers or by accident. They encountered them as problems. More importantly, they often encountered them at a moment when a perfectly acceptable solution already existed.
This is one of the most interesting facts about the world of Haute Horlogerie.
Progress begins not only because there was no answer, but it also begins because someone suspects the existing answer is incomplete.
The first chapters of 40 Moments with Bernhard explore this territory long before innovation, independence, or chronometry become central themes. What emerges is not a fascination with finished watches, but a growing awareness that the same question can produce more than one answer.
A grandfather's watch raises a question. A book provides an answer, then introduces another question. Historical watches reveal solutions that differ from modern explanations. Flea markets, museums, workshops, and collections preserve evidence of previous attempts to solve similar problems.
Together, they point toward a simple conclusion:
An accepted solution is not necessarily the only solution.
That idea sounds obvious when stated directly, but it fundamentally changes a person's point of view.
The objective is no longer understanding how something works. The objective becomes understanding why it works that way and not another.
The watch stops being the answer. It becomes evidence.
Then reality enters the picture.
Alternative answers are easy to discuss in theory. Pursuing them is considerably harder.
A repair waiting on the workbench does not care how convincing an idea sounds in a book. Components either work or do not. Instructions are not always available. Workshops have little patience for theory that cannot survive practice.
One of the recurring themes in Bernhard's recollections from this period is the realization that understanding could not simply be received. It had to be assembled.
Books provided one source of knowledge. Repair work provided another. Senior watchmakers contributed perspectives unavailable in manuals. Historical mechanisms revealed evidence that books alone could not provide.
But none was sufficient. And so the search must continue.
'Wacthmaking' by George Daniels (Image © Blackbough.co.uk)
Long before independence, before chronometric research, and before the innovations that would later define Bernhard's work, a foundation was already forming. Not a collection of answers, but a particular way of approaching them:
The suspicion that another answer may exist.
And the acceptance that proving it requires considerably more effort than imagining it.
Bernhard Lederer working at the Neuchâtel workshop.
The first chapters of 40 Moments with Bernhard are not concerned with conclusions. They explore an earlier stage of the journey, when possibilities remain open, and understanding is still being built.
